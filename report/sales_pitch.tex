\subsection{The Challenge}
Pension funds and institutional investors face growing pressure to reduce their carbon
footprint --- from regulators, from clients, and from the Paris Agreement. The natural
fear is that going green means giving up returns. Our results, based on 12 years of
real data, tell a more encouraging story.

\subsection{Two Strategies, One Clear Goal}
We tested two long-only portfolios on 633 European equities, rebalanced every year
from 2014 to 2025. Both target a 50\%+ carbon reduction relative to the market
benchmark, with no derivatives, no complex instruments:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Static 50\% cap} ($P^{vw}(0.5)$): halves the carbon footprint of
    the benchmark from day one, while staying as close as possible to market returns.
    \item \textbf{Net-Zero glide path} ($P^{vw}(NZ)$): reduces carbon by 10\% every
    year, fully aligned with the EU Climate Transition Benchmark regulation.
\end{itemize}

A 72\% carbon reduction for a cost of just 32 bp per year. To put that in perspective:
on a \$100M portfolio, that is \$320,000 per year --- a price most clients consider
reasonable for full EU CTB alignment.

\subsection{Why It Works}
The carbon footprint of a portfolio is not spread evenly. Just 10 firms --- including
Holcim, RWE, Rio Tinto and Enel --- account for 44\% of the benchmark's total carbon
intensity. By trimming these positions and reallocating to low-carbon names, the
optimiser achieves large carbon reductions with very small deviations from the benchmark.
The European market also helped: under rising EU carbon pricing, the broad index
self-decarbonised by 58\% over the period, which kept the financial cost of the
Net-Zero constraint low --- especially in the early years.

\subsection{Risks We Take Seriously}
We do not hide the limits of this analysis:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Sample dependency.} These results are specific to European equities
    over 2014--2025, a period of strong regulatory tailwinds and tech-led growth.
    The same strategy in a different market or a different decade could show a higher
    financial cost.
    \item \textbf{Scope 3 not included.} We only use Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions.
    Scope 3 (supply chain) is the largest source for many sectors and is not yet
    fully covered by data providers.
    \item \textbf{Transaction costs.} Annual rebalancing generates turnover. Real
    implementation costs must be assessed before going live.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Our Recommendation}
For a client seeking immediate impact, the \textbf{static 50\% cap} delivers a
verified carbon reduction from year one at minimal cost. For a client seeking
long-term regulatory alignment, the \textbf{Net-Zero glide path} is the strongest
choice: it ends the horizon with 72\% less carbon than the benchmark, at a cost
of 32 bp per year. Both strategies are transparent, rules-based, and ready to implement.
